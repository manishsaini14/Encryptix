Mahatma Gandhi

DOB : 2 October 1869

Mahatma Gandhi: An Overview

About

Mahatma Gandhi, born Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat, India, is one of the most iconic leaders in world history. He is best known for his leadership in the Indian independence movement against British colonial rule and his philosophy of nonviolent resistance, or Satyagraha.

Early Life and Education

Gandhi was born into a Hindu family. His father, Karamchand Gandhi, was a government official, and his mother, Putlibai, was a deeply religious woman whose spirituality influenced Gandhi's outlook on life. Gandhi studied law in London, attending University College London, and became a barrister in 1891.

Time in South Africa

In 1893, Gandhi went to South Africa to work as a legal representative for an Indian firm. He stayed there for 21 years, where he faced intense racial discrimination. The injustice against Indians and Africans under colonial rule shaped his views on civil rights and equality. Gandhi began organizing the Indian community in South Africa to protest unjust laws, which led to his development of Satyagraha, a philosophy of nonviolent resistance to tyranny.

Role in Indian Independence Movement

After returning to India in 1915, Gandhi became a prominent figure in the struggle for independence from British rule. He led several major campaigns that shook the foundation of the British Empire:

1. Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922):
Gandhi called on Indians to boycott British goods, institutions, and services, promoting the use of Khadi (hand-spun cloth) as a symbol of self-reliance.

2. Salt March (1930):
Also known as the Dandi March, this was one of Gandhi’s most famous acts of civil disobedience. He marched 240 miles to the Arabian Sea to make salt, defying British laws that imposed a tax on salt production.

3. Quit India Movement (1942):
Gandhi and the Indian National Congress launched this campaign demanding an end to British rule. Despite the arrests of Gandhi and many leaders, the movement intensified the call for independence.

Philosophy of Nonviolence

Gandhi’s principle of Ahimsa (nonviolence) was central to his approach. He believed that oppression could be resisted without violence, and that moral force was stronger than physical force. His philosophy influenced civil rights movements across the world, including those led by Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela .

Personal Life and Beliefs

Gandhi lived simply, wearing a traditional dhoti and shawl, often spun by his own hands using a charkha (spinning wheel). He promoted self-sufficiency, rural development, and cleanliness, and he sought to eradicate the caste system, especially focusing on the upliftment of the Harijans (people from the lowest castes, whom he referred to as "Children of God"). Gandhi also fasted on several occasions to promote unity and peace between Hindus and Muslims in India.

Assassination

On January 30, 1948, Gandhi was assassinated by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist, who opposed Gandhi’s advocacy for Hindu-Muslim unity and his perceived concessions to Muslims during the partition of India.

Legacy

Mahatma Gandhi's legacy of nonviolent resistance and his role in securing India's independence made him a global symbol of peace, tolerance, and justice. His birth anniversary, October 2, is observed as Gandhi Jayanti in India and is also recognized internationally as the International Day of Non-Violence.
